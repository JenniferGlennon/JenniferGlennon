What We Do

Long before infants can talk, eye tracking can provide detailed information about how they perceive and learn about the world. This provides a unique oportunity to identify early risk factors in the emergence of developmental atypicalities. For instance, children at early risk of developing autism will show unique patterns of visual perception that can be captured using eye tracking technologies.

Electroencephalography (EEG) is a test that measures the electrical activity in the brain, called brain waves. It measures brain waves through small button electrodes that are placed on the scalp and is a completey non-invasive way of studying how children process the world around them. It also enables us, as scientists, to explore the relationship between brain function and behaviour in childhood and throughout development.

Infant near-infrared spectrometry (NIRS) is a way of measuring babies brain activity by recording the colour, and therefore the oxygenation of blood. It can give us clues about the functional activation of each region of the brain and how brain activity patterns develop early in life. Functional and structural connectivity between brain regions can also be analysed through complex computing methods based on correlations among regions.
